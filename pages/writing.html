Preventing a flash war: Countering the risk of AI-driven escalation on the battlefield

Kyle Brown, Julian Gould, Jesse Hamilton

This post on the Penn Center for Ethics and the Rule of Law blog, coauthored with Lieutenant Colonel Kyle Brown and professor Jesse Hamilton, draws a parallel between the 2010 Flash Crash in financial markets and the emerging risks of escalation in AI-enabled warfare.

We argue that human oversight alone may be insufficient to prevent rapid, unpredictable escalation and propose battlefield “circuit breakers” as a regulatory safeguard. Drawing on lessons from finance and national security strategy, the piece outlines how the United States could lead in developing international mechanisms to prevent unintended AI-driven conflict.

Proofs as Rhetoric

Julian Gould

In Proofs as Rhetoric, I argue that mathematical proofs, as we write and communicate them, are not purely formal deductions but acts of persuasion—rhetorical constructions aimed at convincing an audience that a result is true. Drawing on classical rhetorical concepts like logos, ethos, and pathos, I explore how proofs depend not only on logical soundness, but also on the credibility of the author and the emotional resonance of the argument.

These ideas have practical consequences for both research and teaching. In research, we must tailor our proofs to the expectations and norms of different mathematical communities. In the classroom, recognizing the rhetorical nature of student proofs changes how we assess clarity, style, and understanding.

Ultimately, I advocate for a broader recognition of rhetoric as a central component of mathematical practice and pedagogy.

Customary International Humanitarian Law and Greedy Algorithms

Julian Gould

Rule 14 of Customary International Humanitarian Law aims to strike a balance between minimizing harm to civilian lives and infrastructure and permitting military operations in or near civilian areas. In practice, this often resembles a greedy algorithm: each individual attack must, in expectation, yield greater military advantage than civilian harm.

But greedy algorithms are, by nature, short-sighted. They fail to account for path dependencies and are prone to getting stuck in locally—but not globally—optimal outcomes. This essay, written in reflection on the Ethical and Legal Dilemmas of Autonomous Weapons in War and National Security conference, explores the limitations of proportionality as framed by Rule 14, the role of path dependency, and how these issues intersect with AI-driven decision-making. The piece concludes with some proposed alternatives to Rule 14.

Moral Mereology

Julian Gould

In mathematics, local-to-global (L2G) and global-to-local (G2L) principles are powerful results concerning structures and their constituent parts. At heart, an L2G principle asserts that if a structure can be decomposed into pieces, and each piece satisfies a certain property, then the whole structure does as well. Conversely, a G2L principle states that if the entire structure satisfies a property, then each of its parts must too.

Many contentious issues in moral and political philosophy can be fruitfully analyzed through the lens of L2G and G2L reasoning. This essay explores several such examples—including distributional justice, plural obligations, the slogan "ACAB," mosaic theory in intelligence analysis, and institutional racism. I argue that a unifying theory theory of local-to-global and global-to-local principles in ethics is needed to clarify the relationships among these seemingly disparate debates.

Stop Having Opinions

Julian Gould

Social media and mass media have radically expanded not just the range of acceptable opinions (the Overton Window), but also the number of topics on which we're expected to have a view. Call it the Overton Load. We're expected to have opinions on everything from housing policy to international trade deals to local school board decisions halfway across the country. If you don't have an opinion on these matters, it's interpretted as a character flaw.

This essay explores how the pressure to opine on every issue leads to social tension, cognitive overload, and worse policy outcomes. It's a case for epistemic humility; we should recognize our limitations to deeply understand and analyze all of the issues du jour. Most of us are wrong about most things. Why pretend otherwise?

Are we fit to SAIL?

Julian Gould

The University of Pennsylvania is replacing traditional calculus lectures with “SAIL”—Structured Active In-class Learning. It's a flipped classroom model that, in theory, maximizes engagement and improves learning outcomes. I don't dispute the theory. But I'm skeptical it will work in practice.

This essay explores three structural obstacles to successful SAIL implementation: free rider problems (students relying on others to do the prep work), unstable equilibria (once the course slides into lecture mode it doesn't come back), and principal-agent problems (instructor incentives are not aligned with student learning outcomes). Taken together, these form a kind of pedagogical Bermuda Triangle. Some SAIL classes will thrive. Most, I suspect, will sink into being lectures in disguise.

Induction and Co-Induction

Julian Gould

This is a write-up of a talk I gave for the Penn Undergraduate Mathematics Society in Spring 2023. It starts with a familiar idea—induction on the natural numbers—and slowly generalizes it until the concept almost disappears. Along the way, we recast induction in terms of initial algebras, define what it means to do induction on structures like lists, and explain how these ideas show up (covertly) in many parts of mathematics.

Then we enter "co world" and turn the arrows around. The second half of the essay explores the dual notion of coinduction via coalgebras, where the focus shifts from construction to observation. We discuss black box systems, terminal coalgebras, and stream differential equations—a coinductive framework for reasoning about infinite sequences.

The Continuum Hypothesis is True in Practice

Julian Gould

Most mathematicians know the standard story: the Continuum Hypothesis (CH) was the first major open question in set theory, placed at the top of Hilbert's famous list in 1900, and ultimately shown to be independent of the ZFC axioms. Is it true? Is it false? Dealer's choice. While historically correct, the standard story is incomplete (as so many tales involving Gödel are). For nearly a century, numerous mathematicians believed CH was true and tried to prove it. One of the most ambitious efforts was the so-called Perfect Set Program.

This essay traces the mathematical and historical arc of the Perfect Set Program–a failed attempt to prove CH by showing that every uncountable subset of the reals contains a perfect subset. While it failed to prove CH, of course, it led to beautiful results and a fascinating partial truth: in most natural settings, uncountable sets of reals do contain perfect subsets. CH may be independent of set theory, but the program left us with the conclusion that it's “true in practice.”
